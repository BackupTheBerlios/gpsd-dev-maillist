On 5/23/05, Remco Treffkorn <remco at rvt.com> wrote:
> ...
> Ever since then, my credo for gpsd was: It may not tell you anything, but it
> will never tell you a lie!
> 
> There are not too many OSS projects that can kill you outright, like gpsd can.
> Sure, it's their own fault, they should not rely on a single source of
> navigation. But people are stupid, and I am (luckily) living proof of that.
> 
> This is why I think, gpsd is unlike any other OSS project, and a more
> conservative approach to QA is justified.

Along with this, I'd like to overstep my authority and speak for all
distributions that make prebuilt packages and say that frequent
releases "just because" are annoying. But shipping bugs is worse, so
bugfix releases are desirable.

That being said, a weekly release makes for a convenient test point:
"all you users - I plan to ship this on friday as version foo. test it
against your current setup". That's what motivated me to build 2.24
wherein I discovered that the zodiac module used the
not-universally-available log2().

If the current bug list is small/empty, let's call it stable, and
don't mess with it any more. It may not have every feature out there,
but at least everything works correctly in a manner that makes sense.
We'll patch it and crank the patchlevel if we do find bugs, but there
should be a feature-frozen stable release that can be used everywhere.
Looking at the various "ports" systems out there, I see that there is
a lot of tolerance for a project with frequent releases, just so long
as much of the activity can be kept constrained to a -devel or
-snapshot branch and regular users can just use the -stable branch.

I'm not opposed to kicking out snapshots, and even calling versioned
development snapshots, but I would also like to see a slow moving
stable track.
