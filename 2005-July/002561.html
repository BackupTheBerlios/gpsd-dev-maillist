> I thought the reported resoution of the GPS clock was only .01sec.

There are several clock signals of interest on gps satellites.  All of
them can be used to work out one's position in 4-space.  The first two
are used by consumer gps gear.  The latter only by survey gear.

        navigation msg:       50 bit/s    20ms
        pseudocode chip rate: ~1 Mbit/s   ~1us
        RF carrier:         ~1.5 Ghz    ~0.6ns 

When one has access to precise phase lock loops the internal clock
accuracy is no longer limited to the clock rate of the input signal.
(Think PPS pulses that only come once per second, but one can
accurately interpolate to high accuracies between pulses.)  The only
constraint is the sharpness of the clocking pulse's edge.  It is
essentially just the sharpness of this edge that limits how much
information one can recover from the pulse train.

Remember, light travels around 30cm in a nanosecond.  If a gps (such
as the SiRF units) can work out their position to 2 meters which is
the distance light travels in ~6 nanoseconds, then someplace they
needed to have figured out the precise time down to that level of
accuracy.  Now unfortunately no gps seems to put out this level of
accuracy on its PPS timekeeping pin, but thats a different story.
