Chris Adams <cmadams at hiwaay.net> writes:

> Once upon a time, Michael R. Davis <mrdvt92 at yahoo.com> said:
>> My company wants to do an experiment with how the GPS/UTC few seconds
>> offset affects things with our system.  Does anyone know how to trick
>> NTP into sliding ahead of UTC by 15 seconds?  FYI there is no GPS time
>> zone which was my first thought.  I think we should add it!  Thanks,
>
> I have time zones with/without leapseconds on my Fedora and RHEL
> systems:
>
> $ TZ=right/US/Central date; TZ=US/Central date
> Wed Feb 24 17:32:11 CST 2010
> Wed Feb 24 17:32:35 CST 2010

That's very funky, and seems kind of wrong wrong - the first isn't the
actual time in CST.  FWIW, on NetBSD 5 I get:

  $ TZ=right/US/Central date; TZ=US/Central date
  Thu Feb 25 13:19:34 GMT 2010
  Thu Feb 25 07:19:34 CST 2010

which means that right/US/Central is not recognized.  CST is defined as
an hour offset from UTC, so it sounds like right/US/Central is probably
constructing a timezone which is the same offset from TAI instead, which
should probably show as CST(TAI) instead.  24 seconds doesn't sound like
the right offset or the right sign, either; I thought TAI was 34 seconds
ahead.

Are these timezones in the standard tzcode package?  NetBSD imports that
regularly, so I would guess they aren't.
