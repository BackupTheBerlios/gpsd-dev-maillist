My point is a soft one, having to to with 'reasonable' expectations about what 
a driver 'should' provide. If you are doing something 'ordinary', something 
that seems 'normal' in the context of what one would 'expect' a driver to do, 
and it does not work, then you might blame the driver. I believe a driver 
should do what a group of 'average' developers 'expects' from it.

For example: Most everybody would agree that a serial driver that requires you 
to write a single character, then wait x milliseconds before the next can be 
written is not good. On the other side of things, almost nobody would blame a 
driver for not wanting to do a 4k write to a 300 baud port in one sitting.

There is no standard (I know of) that dictates what the relationship of buffer 
depth to bandwidth should be. I believe the phrase is "make it reasonable'.

Please note: I am mot saying that this obligates the driver maintainer to do 
anything. This test is just to decide where effort is spent most efficiently.
