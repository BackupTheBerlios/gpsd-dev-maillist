I said three days, but we didn't actually ship for five days. During
most of that time I was working like crazy on getting the
floating-point problemms in the regression tests fixed, not adding
features.  Actually the *only* new feature added in the run-up to 2.35
was Marc Balmer's revised xgps, which I evaluated as a low risk
because it didn't touch the daemon code.

I was also wondering why I didn't see more fix patches.  I'm guessing
you weren't the only dev to miss the release warning.

I agree more time would have been desirable, but I'm not at all sure
it would have prevented the Garmin driver from getting busted -- I suspect
I broke that while trying to fix a regression in the handling of
Evermore packets.  The fundamental problem wasn't time pressure, it
was the fact that the Garmins have to be live-tested after anything
that touches the packet handling, and you didn't know I was touching
that code.

>                                                     We have released
> many bad versions this same way before and keep making the same rush to
> release.

I don't agree.  Or. at least, I think our recent 2.x releases announced as 
stable have been pretty good.  I'm judging this by what I see in my mailbox 
after the releases.  

OTOH, I do want us to coordinate better so we don't ship another release
with regressions in the behavior of the problem children.  "Problem children" 
in this context are the GPS types that can't be tested with a static 
regression load, like the Garmins and the still-broken TNT.

The ideal solution would be for the problem children to be hooked up
to a machine with a test-guest account that devs can ssh into and try
out gpsd builds on.  Would that be possible with your Garmins?
