Yo Eric!

On Tue, 29 Mar 2005, Eric S. Raymond wrote:

> There is something in what you say...but gpsprof is not a novice tool.
> It's a profiling script for people who already have a fair anount of
> technical knoledge about the application domain.

I agree that most of the plot modes are for truly arcane uses.  There
is the one usage of gpsprof that every novice will want to use.  That is
takeing 10k samples from a fixed position to get the best average fix.

There are a few ways to proceed here.  One is to add a switch to
call gprof directly.  Another is provide a simple script to do
the whole task.  Worst case we need to add a complete command line example
in the man page on how to use gpsprof/gnuplot.

It took me a while to realize I needed to use persist, otherwise my
plots where very ephemeral.  THis sort of detail should not be required
for the most basic usage.

> In this case, the Unix way says clearly to prefer the design that
> lends itself to cooperating with other programs.  I had to violate
> that because I didn't know about "e\n", but I wasn't happy about it.


"tar" is the clear embodiment of the Unix way (enough features to
choke a horse and make the man page formidable).  tar has no problem
with flipping between stdout and file out with the [file] or '-'
syntax.  With a properly installed tar it will even go to your tape drive
by default.  Similarly sort, cat, etc. can handle stdio and files.

I am all for pipes and tees, but for some things you just want an simple
command to just do all that is needed.  Sorta like your reasons for moving
to zero config.
