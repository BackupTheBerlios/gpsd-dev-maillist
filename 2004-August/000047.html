> Hamish <hamish_nospam at yahoo.com>:
> > Say in a rescue situation, a single fix which is even an hour or six old
> > may make all the difference. (eg GPS enabled handhelds or yacht
> > navigation systems) 
> > 
> > But maybe it is up to the client to hold on to that, as I don't see a
> > clean way to have gpsd say "here is the latest values, but oh, they are
> > 30 min old." especially when all the different values come from
> > different sentences.
> 
> Exactly.  That's why I emphasized *to that client*.  
> 
> > Regardless of the baud rate issues, I think any timeout should really be
> > 5 sec..
> 
> I'm opposed to that. I want to hold the timeout to a minimum because
> I'm thinking of user convenience in a world where a GPS is just
> another hotplug device.  An "offline" indicator that with a five-second 
> lag is a pain in the butt.
> 

Maybe I'm not catching on, but I expect the software to give me an idea of 
where I am.  GPS units don't work like just another hotplug device.  They 
can take a while to get a lock, and they can lose lock at inconvenient 
plans.  My expectation of the 'right' behavior is to give me the last best 
information.

I much much prefer a six hour old position with a timestamp to no 
position.

The whole issue of 'offline' sort of confuses me.  Basically, we don't 
really have a reliable way to know if the GPS is 'online' or 'offline.'  
But we do know when we got the last useful information from the GPS.  It 
seems to me that the question of whether 1, 2, or 180 seconds is 'on line' 
or 'off line' is best left to the client.

'Here is what I know, and this is when I knew this' is what I want from 
the demon.
